## Trump and Putin Ally Viktor Orban Fights for His Political Life

A hero to the MAGA world and Europe’s nationalists, Hungary’s prime minister faces the toughest electoral challenge of his 16 years in power.

When US Vice President JD Vance arrived in Budapest this week ahead of Hungary’s election, it was a path well-trodden. French far-right leader Marine Le Pen, her Dutch counterpart and Poland’s president have all visited to give a boost to their ally, Viktor Orban.

The vibe in Hungary suggests that Europe’s longest-serving prime minister [needs all the help](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/orban-rallies-far-right-allies-amid-claims-of-russian-meddling?sref=XNB4Nygj) he can get. He’s embroiled in the fight of his political life against a younger challenger who has mobilized support like only Orban could in the past. The outcome of the vote on Sunday will have geopolitical ramifications from Washington to Moscow via the presidential palace in Kyiv.

Since returning to power 16 years ago, Orban has turned Hungary into the continent’s populist agitator, the country in the European Union and NATO that’s both closest to the Kremlin and feted by Donald Trump and the MAGA movement.

As well as the [love-in with Vance](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/vance-picks-fight-with-europe-over-orban-in-election-endorsement), fresh revelations about Budapest’s cozy relations with Moscow have emerged. Bloomberg [reported this week](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/viktor-orban-offered-to-help-vladimir-putin-call-transcript-shows) that Orban told Vladimir Putin during a phone call in October that he was willing to assist the Russian president “in any way,” comparing himself to a mouse aiding a lion from a children’s fable. Orban responded by saying he says the same to Trump.

Opinion [polls show](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/hungarian-opposition-poll-surge-shows-orban-faces-election-loss) he’s on the ropes. Opposition leader Peter Magyar, a former ally of Orban and his ruling Fidesz party, has vowed to shift Hungary back toward the EU mainstream by loosening ties with Russia, tackling corruption, weeding out cronyism and reinstating the independence of the judiciary and media.

Viktor Orban and US Vice President JD Vance embrace at a pre-election rally in Budapest on April 7.Photographer: Akos Stiller/Bloomberg

Magyar’s Tisza party has a steady double-digit lead, with potentially billions of euros of frozen EU funding at stake that Hungary’s struggling $220 billion economy badly needs. Many of Orban’s other opponents are backing Tisza, while [investors are betting](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/traders-line-up-wagers-on-hungary-s-markets-as-election-nears) that if Magyar forms the next government he will reset relations with European allies. The currency, the forint, is trading near to its strongest level against the euro in three years.

A defeat for Orban would also be a huge win for liberal democracies against the allies of Trump who have made Orban’s cause their own and framed their movement as an international force, said Jonathan Eyal, an associate director at the Royal United Services Institute in the UK.

The Hungarian premier showed he could defy the Berlin-Paris-Brussels axis of power and not only get away with it, but keep winning. He was the driving force to unite Europe’s splintered nationalists. His Patriots for Europe is the third-largest group in the European Parliament.

“If the wheel of democracy can really turn completely in a country that looked like it was already scorched earth as far as the government is concerned, that would be very important,” said Eyal. “The psychological significance is huge.”

Orban is amping up his role on the global stage — the well-connected peacemaker in a time of war, a message reinforced by a formidable state media machine and endorsed by Trump.

He [opposes aid for Ukraine](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/defiant-orban-tells-eu-he-won-t-back-down-from-ukraine-aid-veto?sref=XNB4Nygj), blocking a €90 billion ($105 billion) EU loan to Kyiv last month, and has gone all-in on depicting President Volodymyr Zelenskiy as the belligerent, while dismissing hecklers at election rallies as Ukrainian agents.

But while Orban casts himself as a buffer against the war next door, Hungary’s proximity to Russia threatens to make it key to Moscow’s efforts to destabilize Western alliances, according to [Peter Buda](https://x.com/peter1buda), a former Hungarian counter-intelligence officer.

“If Hungary stays on this path, then we have to be prepared that Russia will use us to try to level bigger and bigger blows against others, particularly to undermine NATO,” said Buda. “Russia doesn’t support Hungarian leadership for the sake of it, it does so out of interest.”

Orban, 62, remains a beacon for anti-EU, [anti-LGBTQ+](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-28/hungary-set-to-hold-pride-parade-in-defiance-of-orban-s-ban), anti-immigrant nationalists across Europe, with Hungary vying with Poland over the years as the defender of Christian family values. On his visit this week, Vance slammed the EU for what he claimed was interference in Hungary’s election and hailed Orban at a campaign event as a model leader.

At home, though, he’s struggled to stop a [surge in support](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-28/orban-campaign-shows-cracks-as-anger-mounts-over-spying-scandal) for Magyar, 45, a conservative whose Tisza party has seen its urban and younger support expand and spill into the countryside to traditional Fidesz strongholds. Magyar said the election would be fought neither “in Washington nor in Moscow nor in Brussels” but “on the streets and squares of Hungary.”

Meanwhile, officials and former government insiders from police investigators to competition regulators are using the media to [complain about](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/terminal/TD4E6RT9NJLT) conditions inside the Hungarian state.

Tisza activists campaigning on the streets of Székesfehérvár on April 4Photographer: Akos Stiller/Bloomberg

During Orban’s time in office, Hungary has slumped to the bottom of the rankings for EU members in Transparency International’s annual [Corruption Perceptions Index](https://www.transparency.org/en/countries/hungary) while some in his inner circle have been enriched.

His government [set up an anti-graft office](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-05/hungary-s-orban-agrees-to-set-up-independent-anti-graft-body?sref=XNB4Nygj) in response to EU pressure, [though its chief](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-01-16/hungary-s-anti-corruption-agency-chief-says-orban-s-system-isn-t-corrupt) had complained of lacking powers to effectively combat high-level corruption while staff said they were subjected to political harassment.

Living standards have also failed to keep pace with increases in other countries in Eastern Europe. Per-capita gross domestic product adjusted for local prices has risen to 76% of the EU average. Yet Hungary trails Romania and Poland, while consumption per person adjusted for the cost of living is now behind Bulgaria as well.

“April 12 is a fateful date,” said Gyorgy Waberer, an entrepreneur who grew rich turning a sleepy state haulage company into one of Hungary’s few home-grown multinationals. “You will decide if you want to belong to Europe or to the Russians,” Waberer, who once had [a strategic agreement](https://2010-2014.kormany.hu/en/prime-minister-s-office/news/waberer-s-international-an-example-to-be-followed-by-hungarian-companies) with the Fidesz government and sold a stake in his firm to the [investment group of Orban’s son in law](https://bdpstgroup.hu/en/bdpst-group-invests-in-the-future-of-logistics-the-capital-investment-group-acquires-shares-in-waberers/), said in a post on his Facebook page.

The prime minister has [sought to shore up](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/orban-heads-to-town-squares-after-his-rival-supercharges-support) his party’s core support in smaller towns and cities across Hungary, imploring Fidesz voters to stick with him and unite to keep the country out of the war next door in Ukraine.

Zelenskiy’s face is more visible than Orban’s. He smirks from election posters at bus and tram stops with a slogan about not letting the Ukrainian president have the last laugh. It’s the same trope Orban’s government has used in the past for other perceived enemies of Hungary such as émigré investor and pro-democracy philanthropist George Soros.

“Our real opponent isn’t the Hungarian opposition but their masters,” Orban said in his annual state of the nation address in February. He called Magyar’s Tisza beholden to an EU “empire.”

Magyar and his supporters speak of “ *rendszerváltás*,” or system change, the same phrase for the transition from communism. On March 15, the Hungarian national day when both sides held the biggest set-piece rallies of the campaign, Tisza supporters [filled Budapest’s central avenue](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/orban-and-his-rival-amass-supporters-for-pre-election-showdown) and the square where Orban famously spoke in the summer of 1989 as a pro-democracy student activist.

Peter Magyar greets supporters during a rally in Budapest on March 15, Hungary’s national day.Photographer: Akos Stiller/Bloomberg

One of them, Tibor Farkas, said it’s a now-or-never moment for the country. The 35-year-old voted for Orban when he returned to power in 2010 but now, tired of the state of the economy, the IT manager wants something else.

“If I talk to my relatives, they all feel the same: This is the time we can change something,” said Farkas. “We’d like to have more children and stay here but if the government stays, we will look to leave,” he said, alongside his wife, Renata, a pharmacist, and their 18-month-old daughter. What’s the biggest problem they face? “The system,” said Renata, 32.

While the optics in Budapest suggest Orban is running out of road, he’s hard to beat. Orban [first won power](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/terminal/ETHIJ91A1I70) as a 34-year-old pro-European in 1998 before losing four years later. After returning to power in 2010, he shifted more toward the nationalism that later attracted the MAGA movement. By 2015 he was [erecting a fence](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-09-07/hungary-s-orban-vows-rigor-to-seal-off-border-against-migrants?sref=XNB4Nygj) along Hungary’s southern border to keep out mainly Muslim immigrants.

Orban with US President Bill Clinton prior to their Oval Office meeting in the White House on Oct. 7, 1998.Photographer: Paul J. Richards/AFP/Getty Images

Orban made no secret from the outset of his intention to build an “illiberal democracy” that sought to stay in power indefinitely, and remain in the EU. He used Fidesz’s [two-thirds majority](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-04-03/orban-heads-for-defiant-election-win-to-cement-power-in-hungary) in parliament to extend his influence over all walks of life, from courts to boardrooms and from the theater to [classrooms](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2023-11-23/hungary-orban-s-school-is-training-a-new-generation-of-nationalists).

Steps included passing a new constitution and election rules and appointing political loyalists to the presidency, regulators, judges and even headmasters. A new class of politically-connected super-rich emerged, which included his [boyhood best friend](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-12-17/orban-style-cronyism-turns-gas-fitter-friend-into-a-billionaire?sref=XNB4Nygj) and [his son-in-law](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-03-14/viktor-orban-s-son-in-law-is-building-a-hotel-empire-in-hungary?sref=XNB4Nygj).

For Rikard Veto, an artist attending Orban’s March 15 speech outside the Hungarian Parliament, there’s no other choice: He’s the antiwar candidate who has supported families and pensioners. Veto, in his 70s, acknowledged issues with corruption but said it was far worse in the 1990s when Hungary became a darling of investors by selling state assets.

“I wouldn’t like to be living in this country with a prime minister without experience, without anything,” said Veto, holding a placard of his artwork that has a dove of peace in Orban’s hair. “You have to find someone to give people a better life. This is understood by Orban.”

A rally in support of Hungary's prime minister Viktor Orban and the government in Budapest on March 15.Photographer: Akos Stiller/Bloomberg

Magyar is the most credible challenger Orban has faced, someone who was inside Fidesz. Rather than a big swing in the political pendulum, the election is being fought on whether Hungary continues on Orban’s path.

Tisza has pledged to [not only oust Orban but his system](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/magyar-vows-to-oust-orban-s-allies-if-he-wins-hungary-s-election), something that may require a Fidesz-style margin of victory. If Magyar gets it, he’s said he will cut short the terms of Orban's loyalists including the president, top judges, the chief prosecutor and the heads of state regulatory bodies.

For the EU, it’s a case of anyone but Orban. One official, speaking on condition of anonymity ahead of the vote, said it’s unclear what kind of government a Magyar victory would usher in. The expectation is that he would seek to prove to voters he can get things from Brussels.

Yet European leaders are also figuring out what to do should Orban overcome the deficit in the polls and win a fifth straight term, the official said. One thing is clear: the “gloves will come off” because the situation with Orban isn’t sustainable, the person said.

Balancing east and west has deep roots in Hungarian history, but the strategy has now become a trap after the EU’s patience ran out with Orban, according to [Balazs Trencsenyi](https://people.ceu.edu/balazs_trencsenyi), professor of history and director of the Institute of Advanced Study at Central European University in Budapest.

Orban with Giorgia Meloni at a European Council meeting in Brussels on March 19.Photographer: Simon Wohlfahrt/Bloomberg

“He’s lost the possibility of backtracking,” said Trencsenyi. “His only winning strategy is if Ukraine collapses and Russia wins this geopolitical game.”

Two sources close to the Kremlin say Hungary is a convenient accessory rather than a Russian stooge that can influence EU policy. Orban can hold up aid to Ukraine and if he wins Moscow will be pleased, but ultimately he will only fight for his own interests, one of the people said.

A group of investigative news outlets [published a leaked recording](https://vsquare.org/kremlin-hotline-hungary-colluded-with-russia-to-delist-sanctioned-oligarchs-companies-and-banks/) of a call between Foreign Minister Peter Szijjarto and his Russian counterpart, Sergei Lavrov, about removing the sister of a billionaire from the EU sanctions list.

That followed [one in the Washington Post](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/terminal/TCB5OG33O5C0) earlier in March that Russia’s foreign intelligence service drafted plans to stage an assassination attempt against Orban to boost support for Fidesz. The newspaper also said that Szijjarto would brief Lavrov on the content of meetings between EU foreign ministers. Szijjarto dismissed the report as the work of foreign intelligence services, though has been publicly advertising his frequent conversations with Lavrov.

Pro-Orban election campaign posters in BudapestPhotographer: Akos Stiller/Bloomberg

Supporters and critics alike have long held out hope that Orban would eventually consolidate his system — return freedoms and loosen ties to Russia — if he felt secure enough in power. Not only has that not materialized, Orban’s system grew more authoritarian and his turn away from the EU more radical.

After Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the government set up a Sovereignty Protection Office, helmed by an Orban loyalist, who said in an interview at the time that probing Russian or Chinese influence wasn’t [part of the mission](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-25/hungary-launches-probe-against-graft-watchdog-in-orban-crackdown?sref=XNB4Nygj).

“Orban plays big games,” said Buda, the former counter-intelligence officer. “And that’s why he’ll also lose in a big way — when he does lose.”
